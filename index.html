A disengaged public
Turnout in Syracuse's mayoral election dropped 10% from 2017 to 2021
By Richard Perrins

Syracuse City Hall | Picryl


Voter turnout in Syracuse’s mayoral election was 10% lower in 2021 than it was in 2017, data from the Onondaga County Board of Elections shows.

Incumbent Mayor Ben Walsh won re-election in the Nov. 2 general election over challengers Khalid Bey, a democrat, and the Republican Janet Burman. But only around 28% of Syracuse’s base of registered voters participated in the election.

In 2017, when Walsh initially became mayor, turnout was higher — around 38%.

Mark Brockway, a professor of political science at Syracuse University, said that political fatigue from the 2020 presidential election played a role in the decreased turnout. He also said that the presence of a popular incumbent, like Walsh, often makes voters feel like participation is less important.

“The idea was that the race was already decided and when you have a non-competitive race, turnout is lower across the board,” Brockway said. “It was pretty clear that Mayor Walsh was going to win, and so people didn’t feel like their vote was going to matter.”


Walsh won the 2021 election by almost 6,500 votes. He won by under 4,000 in 2017.

The closest challenger in both elections was a Democrat — Bey in 2021 and Juanita Perez Williams in 2017. A Republican has not served as mayor of the city since Roy Bernardi left office in 2001. Walsh remains the only member of the Independent party to hold the office.

In the summer of 2021, Bey narrowly won the democratic primary for mayor against Michael Greene, who had been endorsed by the party. The difference between the candidates’ vote tallies was just 36 votes. Burman won her Republican primary by 269 votes, the data shows.

According to syracuse.com’s election coverage, the primary elections saw the lowest turnout in Syracuse for 36 years. Only 14% of Democrats and 9% of Republicans cast early or in-person ballots.

Brockway teaches PSC 121, American National Government and Politics, which has an average class enrollment of 150.


One of his class sections could have swayed the entire mayoral election, he said in November.

“If students are concerned about social justice, and they can really throw their weight behind candidates in a way that’s extremely meaningful, the next mayor of Syracuse is going to pay attention to what SU students want,” he said at the time.

In both 2017 and 2021, Syracuse’s 10th Ward, located near the city center, had the lowest rate of participation in the mayoral election. The 17th and 5th Wards, both on the eastern side of the city, had the highest rates of participation in the two elections respectively.

In the late 1930s, insurance agencies across the United States began a process called redlining to assess the likelihood that residents of an area would pay back potential loans.

The criteria for areas of a city ranged from blue, which represented the highest probability that an agency would offer its residents a loan, to red. The system became synonymous with racial discrimination, holding back Black, Jewish and foreign-born people from upwards mobility and home ownership.

According to a 1937 redlining map of Syracuse, what is now the 10th Ward was located in a primarily red and yellow area.


Brockway said underserved areas like the 10th Ward face an uphill battle when it comes to exercising their right to vote because of this historical delineation.

“What people don’t understand is that’s how disenfranchisement works,” Brockway said. “It takes resources, and if you don’t have resources you’re not going to be able to go vote.”

The fact that already underrepresented areas, such as Syracuse’s 10th Ward, are facing ever-decreasing turnout numbers in elections can have ramifications for years to come, Brockway said.

When a candidate like Mayor Walsh receives the majority of his votes in well-off areas like the 17th and 5th Wards, Brockway said, his policy will reflect that dynamic.

During his four-year tenure thus far, Walsh has focused primarily on economic development within the city. In a 2019 “state of the city” address, Walsh outlined his Syracuse Surge plan, which sought to develop underfunded areas of the city technologically.


Measures involved in Syracuse Surge included the 2019 renovation of Central Technical High School, which had been closed since 1975, into a new regional school prioritizing science, technology, engineering, arts and mathematics.

The city has also engaged in data infrastructure measures as part of the surge under Walsh’s administration.

Despite the economic development that has been synonymous with Walsh’s tenure, Brockway said the low turnout is cause for concern for the future of specific neighborhoods that aren’t getting a say in their governance.

“Where’s the city spending its money?” Brockway asked. “This is a place with a huge number of problems that are based on inequality. And is the mayor’s office doing something to lessen that inequality? Or are they doing something to make the people that voted for them happy? And those are not the same thing, often.”
